// Campaign Concept: Soundmap

The 7-Day Drop

A multi-city simultaneous ticket drop built on location, badge tier, and the tension of a random window. Scales from one artist to a full touring strategy.

// Core Idea

Connecting the game
to the real world

Everything inside Soundmap (badges, trades, collections) is a game. The drop mechanic is what makes that game matter in the real world. A single ticket to the right event can drive an insane number of players onto the platform.

Think about a BTS comeback tour. Tickets sell out in minutes. Resale hits $1,000+. Millions of fans have zero realistic path to a ticket. Soundmap becomes that path, but only if you've been playing. That changes the entire value proposition of the app. It's not just a music collector game anymore. It's your best shot at a ticket that doesn't exist anywhere else.

The higher the demand for the ticket, the more powerful the mechanic. One pair of BTS tickets in a city drives more installs and more engagement than any ad campaign at the same cost. And because access is tied to badge tier (earned through real activity), every player who grinds for eligibility becomes a more engaged, more retained user by the time the drop fires.

// Why this is different
Old model
Win ticket giveaway → go to show → nothing. The moment is gone. No artifact. No record. No reason to talk about the app again.
Soundmap model
Win ticket through gameplay → get a shareable winner card to post if you want → attend the show → Legendary Moment unlocks to your profile permanently → share it forever. The concert doesn't end. It becomes part of your collection.
The bigger point: Every other ticket giveaway ends at the door. This one starts there. The Legendary Moment makes Soundmap the only platform where going to a concert makes you more valuable as a player - and gives you something worth sharing that lives in the app forever.
Phase 1: Buy or comp face-value tickets yourself Keep costs tight. One or two pairs per city tied to venue size. A 3,000-cap theater gets one pair. A 10,000-cap gets two. Never spending much per event. Just proving the mechanic works.
Phase 2: Go to management with documented outcomes Screenshots of winners at shows. Engagement numbers from drops. New installs driven by the campaign. The conversation shifts from "can we have comp tickets" to a real pitch.
The pitch "Your sold-out tour just drove thousands of your fans to spend a week grinding on an app, trading your songs, and recruiting their friends to be in range for the drop." That's fan engagement data management teams don't get from Spotify or Instagram. You're not asking for tickets. You're offering a superfan activation campaign and the tickets are just the mechanism.
Who to target first Artists with passionate collector-type fanbases. The kind of fans who buy every vinyl variant and every merch drop. Those management teams already understand that superfans behave differently and are receptive to anything that identifies and rewards them.

Phase 1 · 2020–2021 · Zero budget
Campus ambassador infiltration
Co-founder Alexis Barreyat personally recruited campus ambassadors at French universities, offering equity or early access instead of pay. Reps would post a single BeReal, then go silent. Scarcity created curiosity. The app stayed invite-only, borrowing the psychological playbook of Clubhouse and Gmail. No press releases, no influencer deals.
Invite-only Campus reps Zero ad spend Organic only
Phase 2 · Late 2021–Early 2022 · Peer-to-peer ignition
The university flywheel
Targeted US college campuses with a two-step: seed a small cohort of highly social students, then let network effects do the work. Climbed to #1 at Arizona State, Michigan, and USC before any national presence. Each campus felt like it discovered BeReal on its own. Students recruited their friends because the app only worked if your friend group used it. The mechanism was social obligation, not advertising.
Network effects Bottom-up growth Gen Z focus
Phase 3 · Mid 2022 · Cultural moment
Mainstream breakout without paid media
By July 2022, BeReal hit 10M daily active users and reached #1 globally, with no influencer deals and no paid advertising. Growth was almost entirely driven by TikTok users posting about BeReal organically (often complaining they were "late to post"), which became free cross-promotion. Media coverage followed growth, not the other way around. Vox, NYT, and The Verge all wrote explainers after the surge, accelerating it further.
TikTok spillover Press amplification Word of mouth 10M DAU
2023–2025 · Where it went wrong
Decline, pivot, and acquisition
Retention was weak. Once novelty wore off, daily posting felt like a chore. BeReal introduced a public discovery feed, a celebrities tab, brand accounts, and sponsored RealMojis. These contradicted the core identity and failed to move the needle. Acquired by Voodoo in early 2024 for $800M. Repositioned toward ads and monetization.
Feature drift Retention struggles Identity loss
// The only thing worth copying
BeReal's 2-minute window was engineered FOMO, not a feature
It fired simultaneously for every user, once a day, with no warning. Missing it meant you weren't real. Completing it meant you had proof. That proof was shareable. The window wasn't a UX decision. It was the entire distribution strategy disguised as a product rule.
BeReal's FOMO loop
Notification fires → 2 minutes → miss it and feel behind → catch it and feel authentic → post it → friends see it → they download → loop repeats
Soundmap's FOMO loop
Drop fires → 30 minutes → miss it because your badge was too low → watch someone else win → grind to Platinum → next drop you're ready → loop repeats
The key difference: BeReal's FOMO ended the moment the window closed. There was nothing to show for it the next day. Soundmap's FOMO compounds. Miss the drop, you grind harder. Win the drop, you get a Legendary Moment that sits on your profile forever and makes the next person want it even more. BeReal taught us that engineered urgency works. Soundmap makes it permanent.
